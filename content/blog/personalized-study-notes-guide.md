## Why generic notes fail

Most students rely on shared notes, lecture slides, or hastily compiled
summaries. These resources are designed for someone else's learning style —
not yours. Personalised notes bridge the gap between the material and your
own understanding.

## Know your learning style

Before you start, identify your dominant learning approach:

- **Visual**: Diagrams, concept maps, flowcharts, colour coding
- **Auditory**: Recorded summaries, verbal explanations, discussion prompts
- **Reading/Writing**: Structured outlines, detailed summaries, key term lists
- **Kinesthetic**: Practice problems, case studies, applied examples

Most learners benefit from a blend of these approaches.

## Align with your assessment format

Study notes should mirror how you will be tested:

- **Essay exams**: Thematic summaries, argument maps, key theorists with
  contrasting views
- **Multiple choice**: Fact sheets, definition cards, concept differentiation
- **Problem sets**: Step-by-step solution templates, formula sheets, worked
  examples
- **Presentations**: Talking points, visual aids, Q&A preparation

## Structure for recall

Effective notes use:

1. **Clear headings** that match your syllabus
2. **Bullet points**, not paragraphs
3. **Key terms** bolded or highlighted
4. **Diagrams** where relationships matter
5. **Summary boxes** at the end of each section
6. **Self-test questions** with answers on a separate page

## The Cornell method adapted

Adapt the classic Cornell format for academic subjects:

| Cue Column (left)      | Note-Taking Area (right)          |
|------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Key questions          | Main points                       |
| Vocabulary             | Definitions and examples          |
| Formulas               | Derivations and applications      |
| Theorist names         | Core arguments                    |

Bottom section: 2-3 sentence summary of the page.

## Active recall built in

The best notes include self-assessment prompts:

- "Explain [concept] in your own words"
- "What would happen if [variable] changed?"
- "Compare [Theory A] and [Theory B] on [criterion]"
- "Solve: [practice problem]"
