Creating Personalised Study Notes That Actually Work

May 3, 2026 3 min read

Generic notes rarely help. Learn how to design personalised study materials that match your learning style, course requirements, and assessment format.

Why generic notes fail

Most students rely on shared notes, lecture slides, or hastily compiled summaries. These resources are designed for someone else's learning style — not yours. Personalised notes bridge the gap between the material and your own understanding.

Generic notes treat every learner the same. Effective notes are tailored to how you process, retain, and recall information.

Know your learning style

Before you start, identify your dominant learning approach:

  • Visual: Diagrams, concept maps, flowcharts, colour coding
  • Auditory: Recorded summaries, verbal explanations, discussion prompts
  • Reading/Writing: Structured outlines, detailed summaries, key term lists
  • Kinesthetic: Practice problems, case studies, applied examples

Most learners benefit from a blend of these approaches. If you are unsure of your style, try the same topic using two different formats and compare which one you retained better.

Tip

Combine formats deliberately. A visual learner studying econometrics, for example, might benefit from pairing our step-by-step EViews guide with a hand-drawn flowchart of the regression workflow.

Align with your assessment format

Study notes should mirror how you will be tested:

  • Essay exams: Thematic summaries, argument maps, key theorists with contrasting views
  • Multiple choice: Fact sheets, definition cards, concept differentiation
  • Problem sets: Step-by-step solution templates, formula sheets, worked examples
  • Presentations: Talking points, visual aids, Q&A preparation

A student preparing for a quantitative methods exam should build problem-solving templates. A student preparing for a theory exam should build comparative argument maps. Using the wrong note format for the assessment type is one of the most common — and most avoidable — study mistakes.

Structure for recall

Effective notes use:

  1. Clear headings that match your syllabus
  2. Bullet points, not paragraphs
  3. Key terms bolded or highlighted
  4. Diagrams where relationships matter
  5. Summary boxes at the end of each section
  6. Self-test questions with answers on a separate page
Note

The syllabus is your blueprint. Structure your notes to mirror its headings and subheadings. When exam time comes, you will map questions back to specific note sections instead of hunting through generic material.

The Cornell method adapted

Adapt the classic Cornell format for academic subjects:

| Cue Column (left) | Note-Taking Area (right) |

|---|---|

| Key questions | Main points |

| Vocabulary | Definitions and examples |

| Formulas | Derivations and applications |

| Theorist names | Core arguments |

Bottom section: 2-3 sentence summary of the page.

Active recall built in

The best notes include self-assessment prompts:

  • "Explain [concept] in your own words"
  • "What would happen if [variable] changed?"
  • "Compare [Theory A] and [Theory B] on [criterion]"
  • "Solve: [practice problem]"

Notes you only read are notes you will forget. Notes you question are notes you will remember. Build at least one self-test prompt per major section.
